# **Extinction Risk Dynamics in Mediterranean River Basin Fish Metacommunities: A Case Study of the Guadalquivir River Basin**

## **Metacommunity Theory in Dendritic Ecological Networks**

The conceptualization of ecological communities has undergone a paradigm shift over the last two decades, moving from a view of local communities as closed, niche-determined systems toward the metacommunity framework. A metacommunity is defined as a set of local communities that are linked by the dispersal of multiple potentially interacting species.1 This framework is essential for understanding ecosystems where local community assembly cannot be explained solely by local environmental conditions but is instead heavily influenced by regional processes such as colonization, extinction, and mass effects.1 In the context of freshwater ecology, this theory has gained significant traction because it explicitly embraces processes at multiple spatial scales, providing a foundation for a richer understanding of stream and river communities.1

Riverine systems are archetypical examples of Dendritic Ecological Networks (DENs). Unlike terrestrial landscapes, which are often modeled as a series of patches on a two-dimensional plane, rivers exhibit a hierarchical, branching structure that fundamentally constrains the movement of aquatic organisms.3 This dendritic topology dictates that dispersal is primarily restricted to the watercourse, creating a unique spatial dependency where the position within the network—whether a headwater, a mid-reach, or a mainstem—determines the level of connectivity and the potential for demographic rescue.3 Metacommunity theory as applied to DENs highlights that regional processes (dispersal) depend on the spatial structure of the habitat and the degree of connection to the regional species pool.1

The four main paradigms of metacommunity theory—patch dynamics, species sorting, mass effects, and neutral theory—provide a lens through which researchers can analyze riverine fish dynamics. In the patch dynamics paradigm, community composition is determined by a trade-off between colonization and extinction in equivalent habitat patches.1 Species sorting assumes that habitats are heterogeneous and species are filtered into local communities based on their environmental tolerances, provided dispersal is sufficient.2 Mass effects describe situations where high dispersal allows species to persist in suboptimal habitats, essentially creating source-sink dynamics.1 Finally, the neutral paradigm suggests that species are functionally equivalent and that community structure arises from stochastic processes.1 In dendritic networks, these paradigms often coexist across a gradient: headwaters, being more isolated, are often shaped by species sorting and stochastic extinction, while mainstems, characterized by high connectivity, may exhibit stronger mass effects and greater community synchrony.3

| Metacommunity Paradigm | Primary Mechanism | Connectivity Role | Relevance to DENs |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Patch Dynamics | Colonization-extinction trade-offs | High connectivity allows for rescue effects | Essential for understanding recovery after droughts 1 |
| Species Sorting | Environmental filtering | Dispersal allows species to reach optimal niche | Explains headwater community distinctness 6 |

| Neutral Theory | Stochastic births, deaths, immigration | Dispersal limitation leads to divergence | Applicable in highly isolated headwater fragments 1 |

## **Evolutionary Biogeography and Mediterranean Endemism**

Mediterranean river basins represent some of the most ecologically unique and vulnerable ecosystems on the planet. These basins are recognized as global biodiversity hotspots, characterized by high levels of endemism and a complex evolutionary history shaped by geological isolation and climatic shifts.7 In the Iberian Peninsula, the native ichthyofauna is characterized by a relatively small number of families but an extraordinary degree of diversification at the species level.7 Endemism rates among Iberian freshwater fish are estimated at approximately 80%, with the Cyprinidae, Cobitidae, and Cyprinodontidae families exhibiting the highest proportion of endemic taxa in Europe.9

This high degree of endemism is a direct consequence of the "island-like" nature of Mediterranean river systems. Throughout the Pleistocene, the complex orography of the Mediterranean peninsulas created isolated refugia where fish populations were protected from glacial extremes, leading to allopatric speciation as independent river systems were cut off from the rest of the European continent.8 In these systems, the dendritic network does not just structure local communities; it acts as an evolutionary crucible. Species have evolved specialized life-history traits to survive the characteristic Mediterranean hydrological regime, which features catastrophic winter floods and severe summer droughts.7

The resilience of these species is rooted in their ability to endure high temperatures and low-oxygen conditions in isolated pools during the dry season.9 However, the very isolation that fostered this diversity now poses the greatest threat to its survival. Unlike species in northern temperate regions, Mediterranean fish have limited dispersal capacity to respond to rapid environmental changes because they are confined to discrete basins with no "upstream" escape routes across high-altitude drainage divides.8 When local conditions deteriorate due to climate change or habitat degradation, the risk of global extinction is high because many of these endemics have range-restricted distributions, sometimes occurring in only a single river or lake.8

## **Dendritic Connectivity and the Fragmentation Paradigm**

In riverine metacommunities, connectivity is the "glue" that allows for the persistence of species across the landscape. Structural connectivity refers to the physical arrangement of habitat patches, while functional connectivity relates to the ability of organisms to move through that landscape.5 In dendritic networks, the introduction of barriers—such as dams, weirs, and culverts—fundamentally alters both forms of connectivity, leading to the fragmentation of the network into a series of isolated "islands" of water.5

The impacts of fragmentation are non-neutral; they are dictated by the topology of the network. For example, a single dam on a mainstem river can disconnect an entire upstream sub-basin from the regional species pool, whereas a dam on a headwater stream may only impact a small, localized community.5 To quantify these impacts, ecologists have developed the Dendritic Connectivity Index (DCI), a mathematical measure that assesses the probability of an organism being able to move freely between any two points in the network.5

Fragmentation in Mediterranean DENs is particularly dangerous because it interferes with the natural "pulse" of the system. Native species often rely on seasonal migrations to reach spawning grounds or to escape drying reaches during the summer.7 When these pathways are blocked, populations become genetically isolated, and the probability of demographic rescue after a drought-induced local extinction drops to zero.3 Furthermore, fragmentation often leads to metapopulation synchrony; while asynchrony promotes long-term stability by ensuring that some populations survive even if others fail, high synchrony caused by uniform anthropogenic stressors can lead to simultaneous extinctions across the entire network.3

## **The Guadalquivir Basin A Microcosm of Mediterranean Crisis**

The Guadalquivir River Basin, situated in Southern Spain, serves as a critical case study for understanding extinction risk dynamics in Mediterranean metacommunities. Covering approximately 57,000 ![][image5], it is one of the largest and most biologically significant basins in the Iberian Peninsula.7 Historically, the basin supported a rich array of native fish, with approximately one-third of all Iberian species found within its borders.7 However, the basin has undergone dramatic transformations over the last century, shifting from a natural, flood-prone system to a highly regulated landscape dominated by agriculture and infrastructure.7

Recent biodiversity assessments indicate that the basin is in a state of biological transition. Out of 40 species documented in the basin over the last century, only about 18 to 20 are typically captured in modern field surveys.7 This decline is characterized by a nearly 1:1 ratio between native and exotic species, a phenomenon known as biotic homogenization.7 The conservation status of the native community is alarming: between 50% and 85% of native species in the Guadalquivir are currently categorized as threatened according to IUCN Red List criteria.12

| Basin Segment | Ecological Characteristic | Dominant Threats | Biodiversity Status |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Headwaters | Mountainous; natural forest; low human disturbance | Climate-driven desiccation; fragmentation | Native refugia; lower richness 7 |
| Mid Reaches | Fertile valleys; moderate flow | Intensive agriculture; reservoirs | Highest richness; high invasion risk 7 |
| Lower Reaches | Lowland; slow water; saline influences | Pollution; extreme habitat transformation; exotics | High degradation; exotic dominance 7 |
| Reservoirs | Artificial lentic habitats | Water abstraction; flow regulation | Source of invasive species 7 |

The spatial distribution of biodiversity in the Guadalquivir does not follow the classic "longitudinal increase" pattern.7 Instead, the highest species richness is often found in the mid-reaches, where habitats are relatively stable and connected. The lower reaches, which should naturally support high diversity, are often the most degraded due to high pollution levels, habitat loss, and the overwhelming presence of invasive species such as the wels catfish (*Silurus glanis*) and the black bullhead (*Ameiurus melas*).7

## **Climate Change and the Intensification of Hydrological Stress**

Climate change is not a future threat for the Guadalquivir Basin; it is a present reality. Southern Spain is one of the regions most severely affected by global warming, with summer temperatures already having increased by 1.5°C—a rate that outpaces the global average by 20%.13 Projections for the mid-21st century suggest that these trends will only accelerate, with seasonal irrigation needs expected to increase by 15% to 20% as temperatures rise and the landscape becomes more arid.13

The hydrological consequences of this warming are profound. Total annual rainfall is projected to decrease by 14% to 23% by 2050 compared to the 1971-2000 reference period.17 Under a 3°C warming scenario, available water resources in the Guadalquivir could be reduced by as much as 34%.13 For a river system already under "severe pressure" from irrigated agriculture (which accounts for over 25% of the total cropped area in the basin), these reductions threaten to collapse the remaining aquatic habitats.13

For fish metacommunities, the most critical impact of climate change is the intensification of the drought-flood cycle. As droughts become longer and more frequent, the "pool" phase of the Mediterranean river cycle will expand, potentially leading to the complete dry-out of headwater streams.9 This desiccation eliminates the very refugia that native species rely on for survival. Furthermore, the rising water temperatures and decreasing levels of dissolved oxygen in these isolated pools create physiological barriers to survival, particularly for stenothermic native species that cannot adapt to the heat.16

| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Summer Temperature | \+1.5°C | \+0.7°C per decade | Thermal stress; reduced oxygen 13 |

## **Synergistic Impacts Warming and the Invasional Meltdown**

The extinction risk for Mediterranean fish is rarely driven by a single factor but is instead the result of synergistic interactions between multiple stressors. The most potent synergy in the Guadalquivir is the interaction between climate change and biological invasions.10 As warming water temperatures stress native species, they often create conditions that favor invasive generalists from warmer climates. This process can lead to an "invasional meltdown," where the presence of one invasive species facilitates the establishment and expansion of others, further accelerating the decline of native biodiversity.10

Reservoirs play a pivotal role in this synergy. These artificial structures provide stable, lentic habitats that mimic the native environments of many invasive fish species, such as the largemouth bass (*Micropterus salmoides*) and various limnophilous cyprinids.7 Without these reservoirs, many invasive species would struggle to survive the extreme hydrological variability of Mediterranean rivers. Instead, reservoirs act as "exotic species reservoirs," providing a constant source of immigrants that can colonize the surrounding river network, especially during high-flow events that bypass the dams.7

| Stressor A | Stressor B | Combined Effect (Synergy) |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Rising Temperature | Invasive Predation | Natives are metabolically stressed, making them easier prey 10 |
| Habitat Fragmentation | Drought Desiccation | Fish cannot migrate to find permanent water 11 |
| Reservoir Construction | Invasive Expansion | Reservoirs act as sources for downstream invasion 7 |
| Pollution | Species Sorting | Only resistant (often invasive) species persist 7 |

Invasive species such as the mosquitofish (*Gambusia holbrooki*) have demonstrated remarkable adaptive capacity, following "boom and bust" population dynamics that allow them to rapidly exploit disturbed habitats.24 In the Guadalquivir, the introduction of the European catfish (*Silurus glanis*)—an apex predator capable of reaching massive sizes—poses a new and severe threat to regional wildlife.7 The presence of such predators in the slow-moving, degraded waters of the lower Guadalquivir and the Doñana National Park creates a "predation trap" for native endemics that have no historical experience with such large-scale predators.7

## **Modeling Extinction Risk Tools for Practitioners**

To address the complexity of these threats, there is an urgent need for advanced modeling tools that can help practitioners predict extinction risks and assess the effectiveness of conservation interventions. Traditionally, species distribution models (SDMs) have been used to map habitat suitability, but these often fail to account for the dynamic, multi-scale processes that define metacommunities.25 Modern approaches are now integrating spatially explicit simulations, Bayesian networks, and expert-based Decision Support Systems (DSS) to provide a more holistic view of extinction risk.26

One such tool is the BESTEL-FCM framework, which integrates Biological, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental, and Legal factors into a Fuzzy Cognitive Mapping (FCM) model.22 This system allows stakeholders to simulate policy scenarios—such as dam removals or stricter pollution regulations—and evaluate their impact on species' extinction risk.27 For example, simulations have shown that while dam removal can significantly reduce risk by restoring connectivity, these gains can be completely offset if climate change is left unchecked.22

Another significant advancement is the coupling of spatio-temporal modeling tools like VAST ("Vector Autoregressive Spatio-Temporal") with Red List assessment models like JARA ("Just Another Red List Assessment").28 This framework allows for the characterization of uncertainty in extinction risk assessments by propagating scientific survey data through a probabilistic model.28 In the North Sea, this approach has successfully identified species in urgent need of research and conservation, and it holds great promise for application in Mediterranean river basins where data is often sparse and uncertain.28

| Modeling Approach | Specific Tool | Primary Output | Application |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Simulation | ISIS-Fish | Multi-species/multi-fleet dynamics | Evaluating management measures 29 |
| Spatio-Temporal | VAST-JARA | Probabilistic Red List status | Quantifying extinction risk uncertainty 28 |
| Decision Support | BESTEL-FCM | Policy scenario evaluation | Identifying dominant climate stressors 22 |
| Habitat Suitability | InVEST | Spatial trade-offs (e.g., carbon vs. bio) | Prioritizing restoration areas 30 |

These models provide "virtual laboratories" where managers can test hypotheses before implementing costly on-the-ground actions.29 However, the utility of these tools is often limited by a lack of feedback from practitioners to modellers.25 To be effective, models must be tailored to specific decision-making contexts, such as the identification of critical habitats or the selection of sites for the translocation of endangered species.25

## **Conservation Strategy and Policy Alignment**

The conservation of the Guadalquivir’s ichthyofauna is not only a biological imperative but a legal and strategic one. Spain has committed to several high-level initiatives aimed at restoring its river systems, including the Spanish National Strategy for River Restoration 2022-2030 and the National Plan for Adaptation to Climate Change (PNACC) 2021-2030.31 These strategies are aligned with European policies such as the EU Biodiversity Strategy for 2030, which mandates the restoration of at least 30% of degraded habitats.33

The National River Restoration Strategy is particularly ambitious, aiming to recover and maintain the ecological health of rivers by fostering their natural structure and functions.34 Key objectives include:

1. **Restoring Fluvial Continuity:** Spain expects to recover 3,000 km of the 25,000 km target set by the EU for 2030\.32  
2. **Expanding River Nature Reserves:** The goal is to reach a network of more than 3,500 km of strictly protected river nature reserves.33  
3. **Nature-Based Solutions:** Implementing meander restoration and floodplain expansion to minimize flood risk while enhancing biodiversity.32

| Strategy Component | Target/Metric | Alignment | Impact on Guadalquivir |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| Fluvial Continuity | 3,000 km (National) | EU Biodiversity 2030 | Barrier removal in mid-reaches 32 |
| Nature Reserves | \>3,500 km (National) | Water Framework Directive | Protection of headwater refugia 33 |
| Efficiency in Use | 10% improvement | Circular Economy Strategy | Reducing irrigation-induced stress 35 |
| Wetland Recovery | 20,000 hectares | Ramsar Convention | Restoration of Doñana marshes 33 |

In the Guadalquivir Basin, these objectives are being operationalized through specific restoration projects, such as those in the Segura and Guadiamar sub-basins.9 The Guadiamar, in particular, highlights the complexity of restoration; after a catastrophic mine spill in 1998, a massive recovery plan was undertaken, but the basin remains a source of invasive species despite the physical habitat restoration.9 This underscores a critical lesson: habitat restoration alone is insufficient if the regional metacommunity is dominated by exotic species. Restoration must be coupled with active management of invasive populations to ensure that native species can successfully recolonize the recovered reaches.7

## **Conclusion Synthesis and Actionable Recommendations**

The extinction risk dynamics of the Guadalquivir River Basin serve as a stark warning for Mediterranean river systems worldwide. The interaction of a naturally constrained dendritic topology with rapid climate warming, extensive fragmentation, and biological invasions has created a "perfect storm" that threatens the very existence of one of Europe’s most unique biotas. The transition from a native-dominated system to one of biotic homogenization is already well underway, and the window for effective intervention is closing.

The research justifies an urgent, multi-pronged approach to conservation. First, the maintenance of hydrological continuity must be prioritized; barrier removal is essential to allow native species to migrate and escape local stressors. Second, climate adaptation must focus on the protection of "thermal refugia" in headwaters, ensuring that these areas do not dry out during increasingly severe summers. Third, the management of invasive species must move beyond a site-by-site basis to a basin-wide strategy that recognizes reservoirs as major source areas for exotic expansion.

Finally, the development and deployment of spatially explicit modeling tools are critical for providing practitioners with the evidence base needed for targeted conservation. These models must integrate not only biological data but also the socio-economic drivers—such as agricultural water demand—that dictate the hydrological reality of the basin. By aligning local management actions with the strategic goals of the Spanish National Strategy for River Restoration, there remains a path to preserve the biological heritage of the Guadalquivir. However, this path requires a fundamental shift in how we value and manage these vulnerable dendritic metacommunities, moving from a paradigm of exploitation to one of resilience and restoration.
